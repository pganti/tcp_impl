Please excuse my interjection. As a long time EE and network
consultant, I've been amazed at how long some of the myths you mention
have lasted (e.g., 37% is Enet's load limit). It's common practice, for
instance, in well-designed Ethernets, to run at 70% 24 hrs/day with no
issue -- one of the most famous Wall St. trading companies has done that
for years. Part of the reason why this is not commonly understood is
that in the '70s IBM was not about to license anything from Xerox. Now,
of course, they can't build Enet interfaces fast enough.
The problems with ring systems were discussed well in ACM papers around
November 95/96, and ATM's problems are just becoming evident to its
guinea pigs. As a consultant, of course, that means money for me, but
there is some disappointment that marketing at IBM, Fore and elsewhere
over the years has been able to waste others' time and money on
inappropriate technology.

For those who've been mislead by Miller's and other texts, by authors
who misread either each other or the original IEEE 802 committee's
simulation results, I've attached the DEC Western Research Lab report
from '88 (Postscript), where it was clearly demonstrated by one of
Enet's inventors that 90% throughput (not just load), with minor
collision levels, is quite easy. Sorry for the size, but it's an
important document -- show your children. The earlier IEEE simulations
actually demostrated, to the observant, that Enet gets better than ring
systems as the number of nodes increases (see attached Powerpoint
version) -- CSMA/CD is always quicker at resolving contention than is
token passing. We probably should have it right by now, after 20 years
experience with a technology. I hope these comments are useful to
someone.

> It's the same evidence that has been around for decades. More, it is the
> same issue that is a root of the TLD DNS nonsense infesting the main IETF
> list. Many people are unable to conceive of a working distributed,
> cooperative system, whether in politics, business, or network packets.
> As a result, they either demand a central commission to run it or rush to
> exploit its imagined weaknesses. Thus, you get the ancient IBM token ring
> claims that CSMA/CD does not work (e.g. the 30% silliness), because mere
> statistics are in charge on an Ethernet. Then came the FDDI claims that
> CSMA/CD does not work, followed the 100VG-AnyLAN stories. Those were
> classic, since 100VG's claim to virtue was its central controller, and
> its most telling characteristic was significantly lower throughput than
> 100BT. About the same time came the claims of ATM gurus and sundry
> would-be politicians that IP and the Internet do not work technically or
> politically.
>
> > | >1. The problem with congestion unresponsive traffic
> > | >is becoming increasingly serious as people find
> > | >smart ways to cheat the net.
>
> That's not likely to be a problem for at least a while. The vast majority
> are prevented from play such games by their tiny, <=128 kbit/sec
> connections. The few that have connections fat enough to do significant
> harm would be noticed and handled administratively, if for some reason
> they forget the tragedy of the commons. Any PC users that want to break
> slow-start can tell their browsers to use 40 instead of 2-4 connections
> when talking to the typical web page with its gazillions of blinking ads
> and banners. They don't, because it does them no good, because they're
> throttled by 30 kbps modem links.
>
> ATM is no more required by the theoretical existance of naughty TCP
> installations than analogous machinery is required by the proven existence
> of SYN-bombing, echo-port attacks, etc. If it makes sense to use ATM
> because someone might cheat on slow-start, then it surely makes more sense
> to have every router in the core detect and filter streams of SYNs, packets
> from port 7 to port 7, or packets with surprising source addresses.
>
> Vernon Schryver vjs@rhyolite.com
