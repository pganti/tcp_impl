> From: "David S. Miller" <davem@dm.cobaltmicro.com>
> To: vjs@calcite.rhyolite.com
> CC: craig@aland.bbn.com, vishnu@cs.ucr.edu, mallman@lerc.nasa.gov,
> sob@harvard.edu, tcp-impl@cthulhu.engr.sgi.com, vern@ee.lbl.gov

> The main issue is that if your pipe to the Internet cannot carry 4
> TCP streams, then you would be wise to reduce the number of streams
> that that your WWW browser opens simultaneously. The reason for 4
> TCP streams is in large part due to the initial start-up problem
> that the proposal is intended to help.
>
> Firstly, it is not a configurable parameter for most browsers I know
> of, and can't be changed unless you have the source to the browser.

On the contrary, it is configurable in Netscape for both Unix and Win95.
I can't see where to set it in Internet Explorer on Win95, but that
probably proves more about me than IE.

Moreover, since the proposed change to the initial window size is not
configurable, you would need new HTTP PC client to implement it, and it
seems reasonable that when (and if) your browser vendor changes the initial
window size in the TCP stack it ships, it will also change the default
number of TCP streams.

> Secondly, I was under the impression that the 4 connections trick
> employed by web browsers was classified by this working group as
> incorrect behavior and one that provokes congestion in the internet?

I've been watching the tcp-impl mailing list for some time, but do not
recall any such simply statement. There have been statements deprecating
the 4 link trick (or higher--I've found larger numbers useful in some
situations), but I do not recall any indication that there is consensus
that the 4 line trick is bad given HTTP as currently deployed.

> Also note that the change is NOT a factor of 4. As has been noted
> repeatedly, one of the most common family of systems (particularly
> those used for WWW servers) has for years started with 2, so that
> the change is a factor of 2.
>
> And I was very happy when this working group finally pointed this out
> in the known problems working draft, and classified it as a bug.

That is a purely political statement given the proposal.

Why didn't you respond to the technical substance of my response to your
objection? Technically, just how is it that in your situation you would
be losing 12KBytes given the proposed change, and why wouldn't you make
the changes that are obviously necessary regardless of whether the proposal
is implemented near you, especially to increase the buffering on the ISP
side of your modems to 16KBytes?

Vernon Schryver vjs@rhyolite.com
