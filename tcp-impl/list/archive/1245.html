> From: alan@lxorguk.ukuu.org.uk (Alan Cox)

> > today. Isn't it true essentially all TCP traffic is HTTP, SMTP, FTP,
> > SNMP, DNS, and POP/IMAP? Which of those has application-layer keepalives
>
> Most IMAP, POP3 has long idle periods, SMTP has idle periods - with the
> connection cache in sendmail quite substantial ones

How long are those IMAP/POP idle periods? Are they ever 2 hours?
If the keepalive interval were 15 minutes, what is the probability
that a keepalive would be wasted bandwidth on an idle IMAP/POP connection
and what is the probability that it would not be a waste by killing
a dead connection?

How long is a sendmail connection cached? I thought it was no more than
a few minutes. The default BSD keepalive interval is 2 hours. I've never
seen sendmail cache an SMTP connection for a fraction that long, but I
have seen stale, dead STMP connections. There is the non-standard sendmail
application layer timeout. If you set it to less than 2 hours, it will
fire before the TCP keepalive.

> And the more you lower the timer the more applications fall into the
> category of reasons not to do it.

I do not understand that reasoning. Yes, in the best of all possible
worlds, it might be that all applications would have keepalives. I'm not
convinced that you would not then complain even more loudly about wasted
bandwdith al la the complaints about such things as the Appletalk 15-second
babble, but never mind. The fact is that applications do not have
keepalives.

> What Im hearing from you is 90% hot air and 10% "http needs fixing". Fine
> fix http

How do you propose to fix the many existing HTTP clients to not croak
when they receive your new HTTP keepalive?

As for hot air ... well ... what have you been telling the people you know
who have been having trouble with stale, dead TCP connections for the last
15+ years? (since at least rlogin made keepalives handy) How have the
LINUX systems with merely 1,000,000 HTTP hits/day coped without TCP
keepalives? Since I trust you are not talking through your hat, you must
have an effective alternative. It would be swell to hear your alternative
to setting the TCP keepalive interval to values even I find silly.

It is a fact is that applications today do not have keepalives. It fits
the definition of clinical insanity to act contrary to that fact. Merely
talking as if that fact did not exist is not nececssarily evidence of
insanity, but it is not evidence of anything good.
