> From: alan@lxorguk.ukuu.org.uk (Alan Cox)

> ...
> > I do not understand that reasoning. Yes, in the best of all possible
> > worlds, it might be that all applications would have keepalives. I'm not
>
> Right now the odds of a keepalive on my imap mail connection as I browse
> email occuring is pretty slim - and if it occurs its one packet. Now if
> you had a 5 minute keepalive the odds of it occuring are a lot, and you
> are generating a lot more packets per user for idle "still reading that
> email, gone for a coffee" etc.

5 minutes is below the value that I consider silly or at least
objectionable for a TCP keepalive even for HTTP. Never mind that real
systems servering 10,000,000 hits/day are often forced to use that
particular value to avoid crashing by running out of system resources.
I guess reality is often silly or at least objectionable.

> ...
> > convinced that you would not then complain even more loudly about wasted
> > bandwdith al la the complaints about such things as the Appletalk 15-second
> > babble, but never mind. The fact is that applications do not have
> > keepalives.
>
> Appletalk doesnt run over 1200 and 9600 baud connections

That's irrelevant, and not just because no one today is using 1200 or
9600 bit/sec connections. At least in the U.S., everyone is using at
least v.32bis. The complaints about the Appletalk 15-second (or was it
5-sec?) concerned LANs. Why would you be less offended by ~80 byte
HTTP/TCP/IP keepalives than by 40 byte TCP/IP keepalives, both at the same
rate?

> ...
> The question is whether people should fix the apps or we should accept
> their idiocy and cover for them. Unfortunately I suspect the answer is
> a marketing not a technical one.

If you are offended by TCP keepalives, then work to make it illegal for
the IESG to approve protocols withtout application layer keepalives.
Until you finish that task, you should be insisting in the relevant working
groups that revisions of existing protocols (e.g. SMTP) all be given
application layer keepalives.

Does the new HTTP have a keepalive? I don't think so. If I'm not wrong,
what have you done to fix that particular and currently worst aspect of
the problem?

> Either way getting POSIX to define a socket option for timeout setting
> is probably a good thing (there are valid internal network cases cited
> already where its nobody elses business what people do to their lan)

Why are TCP keepalives a problem out in the open? Are you saying that an
HTTP keepalive would use less bandwidth than the current practice?
