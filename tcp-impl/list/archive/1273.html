> From: braden@ISI.EDU

> ...
> Indeed, I believe that the compromise taken by RFC-1122 is probably the
> right one: be extremely conservative by default, but allow software
> engineers to be less conservative with keep-alives when necessary and
> when fully knowledgable about the possible bad consequences.

I agree that's the right choice. It reflects the consensus of the
community, as well as my personal prejudice. My only disagreement with
is with "extremely"; nothing less than 20 hours sounds extreme to me.

In the future, it would be nice to not encounter overstatements such as
the claim that RFC-1122 all but outlawed TCP keepalives. They add color
and can be useful and effective in converstations among people who know
the score, but they confuse others and can cause them to advocate wrong
and even silly positions. Such statements convey the mistaken impression
that there is no consensus for the use of TCP keepalives in appropriate
circumstances or that there are no appropriate circumstances at all.
(See der Mouse's words today or A.Cox's words yesterday). I think I recall
that 4.2BSD or maybe 4.1c used short keepalives, and maybe on by default,
but I don't think I've met anyone in much more than 10 years that still
thinks that was a good idea. That horse is dead.
