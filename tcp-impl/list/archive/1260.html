Alan Cox writes:
> Im not arguing that der Mouse is doing the right thing in his circumstances.
> But we can't make every random faulty piece of equipment an item to assess
> when evaluating the need for keepalives.

It isn't a "faulty piece of equipment".

The fact is that clients die and leave state hanging in servers. You
connect to a machine and your machine is blown off the net, and from
then until the end of time all those connections hang. An OPTION (not,
*OPTION*) that lets you probe every several hours (HOURS!) with a tiny
packet to make sure that the other side still has some vague idea of
your existance is hardly something that is necessitated by "faulty
equipment". It makes practical sense.

Sure, you might not want it running on every joe random protocol on
every piece of equipment on earth, or even most, but you probably want
it every once in a while, and the argument that it is never reasonable
to make the facility available is just plain nonsense.

> In the firewall case a firewall itself can (and should have) been
> generating its own state checks.

Great, another piece of code to force people to write when it is just
as easy to just leave the, what, dozen lines of code dealing with this
alone?

> The general case "my [link|firewall|proxy|...] doesnt work right therefore
> Im going to cause a lot more traffic"

What "a lot more traffic", bub? As we've noted before, most
implementations don't even send a keepalive until a link has been idle
for *hours*. And as we have noted, your "make the application do it"
solution is no better than this.

I'm sorry to flame about this, but you're arguments just hold no water
at all.

Perry
