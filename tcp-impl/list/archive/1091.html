How and why did that 12K get through the Internet and get dropped? You
have the 28K modem, and you're concerned that the modem has only 4K of
buffering, right? Why is the modem dropping bytes? Why does it not
instead lowering CTS and let the system driving the modem hold the bytes?
The system driving the modem is on the far side of the modem from you
isn't it?

Or instead are you concerned that the system driving the modem has only
4 segments of buffering, and that while it is holding 4 segments for the
modem, it will drop the next 12 segments? If that is the issue, then I
think you have the numbers insiginficantly wrong, since most modems have
more than 1 byte internal buffering. The main issue is that if your pipe
to the Internet cannot carry 4 TCP streams, then you would be wise to
reduce the number of streams that that your WWW browser opens simultaneously.
The reason for 4 TCP streams is in large part due to the initial start-up
problem that the proposal is intended to help.

It would also be wise to get your ISP to ensure that there is a full
window*bandwidth of buffering on your port, which is surely far more than
4 segments. Having only 4 segments worth of buffering is likely to cause
you significant grief regardless of proposed initial window change.

Also note that the change is NOT a factor of 4. As has been noted
repeatedly, one of the most common family of systems (particularly those
used for WWW servers) has for years started with 2, so that the change is
a factor of 2.
